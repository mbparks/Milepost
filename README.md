# MILEPOST

**Field Instrument 003** / Environmental Capture & Journal

A single-file web instrument that takes an environmental fix of the world at the moment of an entry and binds that snapshot to a written observation. Each entry becomes a milepost: a fixed marker along the route you are walking through time.

Version 0.0.25 / Green Shoe Garage / Mountain Maryland

---

## What it captures

When you press **NEW ENTRY**, the instrument queries dozens of distinct sources to assemble a snapshot, running them concurrently after first establishing your location. Each source captures independently and fails gracefully. The capture log shows live status (pending, working, captured, unavailable, key required) for every source.

**Location** via browser geolocation, with IP-based fallback after a 4-second timeout.

**Atmospheric** conditions, temperature, apparent temperature, humidity, dewpoint, pressure, wind (speed, direction, gusts), cloud cover, precipitation, visibility, current UV index, US AQI, pollen counts for six taxa where regionally available (Open-Meteo), and weekly CO₂ concentration at Mauna Loa with year-over-year change (NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory).

**Celestial** sun sign, sun altitude and azimuth, moon phase with illumination percentage and lunar age, sunrise, sunset, civil and astronomical twilight times, day length, currently-active meteor showers with peak-day flagging, next solar or lunar eclipse with countdown, days until the next equinox or solstice, Julian day, plus a recorded URL for the current NASA SDO solar disk image (Meeus algorithms computed locally; sunrise-sunset.org; NASA SDO; client-side meteor/eclipse tables through 2030).

**Space Weather and Orbital** Kp index with geomagnetic activity classification, solar wind speed, plasma density and temperature, aurora visibility probability at your coordinates (the OVATION model's 30-minute forecast), ISS lat/lon/altitude/velocity (NOAA SWPC, WhereTheISS.at).

**Telluric** nearest seismic event within 500km with magnitude, place, depth, and time since; count of nearby M1.5+ events; largest M4+ event globally in the last 24 hours; wildfire thermal anomalies within 500km with distance and fire radiative power (USGS Earthquake Hazards Program, NASA FIRMS).

**Hydrosphere** nearest active USGS stream gauge with discharge in cubic feet per second and gauge height in feet; nearest NOAA tide station with water level relative to MLLW, or "inland" status with distance to the closest coastal station (USGS NWIS, NOAA CO-OPS).

**Biosphere** bird species diversity within 50km over the past 3 days, up to three notable or rare sightings from the past 7 days with locations and counts, and the latest observation (eBird).

**Markets** Dow Jones, S&P 500, NASDAQ Composite, and VIX with day change in points and percent; 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year US Treasury yields with the 10y-5y spread flagged inverted or normal; gold, silver, and WTI crude oil futures with day change (all via Yahoo Finance); BTC, ETH, and XMR vs USD with 24h change (CoinGecko); the Crypto Fear & Greed index (alternative.me); USD exchange rates for EUR, GBP, JPY, and CAD (Frankfurter).

**Cultural Pulse** top song, album, app, podcast, and book from Apple's charts (Apple Marketing Tools RSS); current US box office number-one film with weekend gross (Wikipedia).

**Discourse** the most-read Wikipedia article today with view count and description, Wikipedia's "In the News" headline, the top Hacker News story with score and comment count, and the top r/popular post with subreddit and score (Wikipedia REST API, HN Firebase, Reddit JSON).

**Curio** NASA Astronomy Picture of the Day with title and excerpt, the latest xkcd with title and alt text, the most recent arXiv submission across cs.AI / cs.CL / astro-ph.SR / physics.hist-ph with abstract excerpt, and the most-starred new GitHub repository from the past week (NASA APOD, xkcd JSON, arXiv API, GitHub Search API).

**On This Day** three historical events from this calendar date, drawn from across the centuries (Wikipedia).

Each captured entry is labeled with a small SVG milepost glyph styled after the B&O Railroad's concrete mile markers: rounded-top post, "M" stacked above the entry number, slight weathering gradient, tiny chipped corner.

## Usage

1. Open `milepost.html` in a modern browser, or host the file anywhere.
2. Optionally enter API keys in the **Configuration** panel for the two key-gated sources, and toggle the locomotive whistle if you want sound on capture.
3. Press **NEW ENTRY**.
4. Grant location permission if prompted. The instrument falls back to IP geolocation after 4 seconds if denied or unavailable.
5. The capture log fills in as each source resolves. When capture completes, the calibration stamp drops into place from above, the locomotive whistle plays if enabled, and a small swatch row at the top of the journal area shows the **palette of the day** derived from sun altitude and cloud cover.
6. Write your observation in the **Observation** area below the readings. Optionally adjust the **Mood / Energy / Focus** sliders above the textarea to record an internal-state reading alongside the external one; press **Skip** to leave them unset. Optionally expand **Body / Health Reading** below the textarea to log heart rate, blood pressure, blood oxygen, blood glucose, body temperature, weight, sleep, steps, HRV, and any notes (medications, symptoms, etc.); each field is independently optional and the block stays collapsed by default. Optionally add **Tags** below (comma- or space-separated, with or without `#`) to file the entry under one or more lightweight categories.
7. Save as `.TXT` or `.JSON`, or copy the formatted text to your clipboard. Anything you've written into the observation, tags, or sliders is auto-saved to localStorage as a draft and restored if the page is reloaded mid-entry; the draft clears the moment you save.

### Keyboard shortcuts

`Cmd/Ctrl + Enter` saves the current entry as `.TXT`.
`Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + N` triggers a new capture.

### Session archive

Within a session, completed entries are listed at the bottom of the page. The archive clears on page reload, since persistence is via the file exports.

### Source diagnostic

The companion `milepost-status.html` page probes every data source in parallel and reports green (responding under 3 seconds), yellow (responding but slow), red (failing with the error message shown inline), or grey (skipped because no API key is configured). Useful for verifying the instrument is fully calibrated before relying on it, or for tracking down why a particular section came back unavailable on a recent capture. Cross-links between the three pages (capture, reader, status) appear in each page's header badge.

## API keys (optional)

Two data sources require free API keys. Both are instant signup, no payment, no waiting.

**NASA FIRMS** for wildfire thermal anomaly detection. Request a Map Key at https://firms.modaps.eosdis.nasa.gov/api/map_key/

**eBird** for nearby bird observations. Generate a token at https://ebird.org/api/keygen

Keys are stored in browser localStorage when available, with an in-memory fallback for sandboxed contexts. They never leave your machine except when contacting the respective service during a capture. Without them, those two sources display "add key in settings"; everything else still works.

## Self-hosting a CORS proxy (recommended)

Milepost routes CORS-blocked sources (Yahoo Finance, Stooq, FRED, Frankfurter, Apple charts, arXiv, Piped/Invidious) through a configurable proxy. The default is AllOrigins, with corsproxy.io as a hardcoded fallback. Both are public services, and both have real-world limits:

- **AllOrigins** is free and unauthenticated but gets overloaded during peak hours, where requests start timing out beyond the 10-second per-fetch budget. When it works it's fast; when it doesn't, captures from financial and chart sources go dark.
- **corsproxy.io** maintains an internal blocklist of upstream hosts it classifies as "commercial APIs" and returns HTTP 403 for them. Yahoo Finance, Stooq, Frankfurter, Apple Marketing Tools, and arXiv all currently hit this block.

For reliable captures, deploy your own proxy on **Cloudflare Workers**. The free tier allows 100,000 requests/day, runs at the global edge, and most importantly runs your code so there's no third-party blocklist or rate-limit. A 50-line worker script is included in this repo as `cloudflare-worker-proxy.js`. Deployment takes under a minute:

1. Sign in or create a free account at https://dash.cloudflare.com
2. Open **Workers & Pages**, click **Create**, choose **Start from Hello World**
3. Name the worker (e.g. `milepost-proxy`)
4. Paste the contents of `cloudflare-worker-proxy.js` as the entire worker source
5. Click **Deploy**
6. Copy the worker URL Cloudflare gives you (looks like `https://milepost-proxy.YOUR-NAME.workers.dev`)
7. In Milepost Settings, set **CORS Proxy** to `https://milepost-proxy.YOUR-NAME.workers.dev/?url=` (note the trailing `/?url=` so Milepost can append the encoded target URL)
8. Save Settings, reload `status.html`, run probes

Once your worker is the primary CORS proxy, every previously-failing source should come through. The dual-proxy fallback (yours, then corsproxy.io) gives you belt-and-suspenders for the few hosts where your worker has a transient hiccup.

The worker is open by default (anyone who guesses your URL can use your daily quota), which is usually fine for personal use. If you want to lock it to your own origin, uncomment the `ALLOWED_ORIGINS` block in the worker script.

## Output formats

**Plain text (`.txt`)** is a human-readable field journal page with section rules, aligned columns, and the observation appended after a banner. Filenames are stamped `milepost_YYYYMMDD-HHMM_NNN.txt`.

**JSON (`.json`)** contains the full structured snapshot of every captured signal plus the observation text. Top-level keys are `instrument`, `series`, `version`, and `entry`. The entry's `snapshot` field holds all raw data returned by each source.

## The Reader

`milepost-reader.html` is a companion viewer for the JSON files you have saved. It loads files from your machine and renders them in the same field-instrument aesthetic, with two view modes.

### List view (default)

- A searchable sidebar list of all loaded entries, each marked with its own mini milepost glyph
- A **tag filter strip** below the search box listing every unique tag across the loaded archive with counts, plus an "all" pill. Click a tag to scope the list to entries with that tag; click again or pick "all" to clear. Tags shown on each entry's detail view are themselves clickable jump links into the filter.
- A reading-focused detail panel that puts the observation above the data sections (since by the time you are reading an entry, the observation is what you came back for). Below the observation, entries display their **tags**, the **palette of the day** captured with the entry (four-color swatch row), the **internal-state reading** (Mood / Energy / Focus bars) if the entry had those filled in, and the **body / health reading** (heart rate, blood pressure with clinical category label, blood oxygen, glucose with mmol/L conversion, body temperature in °F and °C, weight in lb and kg, sleep, steps, HRV, and any notes) if any health fields were logged
- A per-entry context map between the metadata blocks and data sections: a small map centered on the capture point with markers for the nearby quakes, fires, stream gauge, tide station, and notable bird sightings the instrument logged. Legend at the foot of the map identifies each marker type.
- An aggregate stats strip across the top: entry count, span in days, distinct locations, total words written, average temperature, total species seen
- Search across observations, locations, weather conditions, cultural picks, news headlines, tags, and filenames
- Arrow-key navigation between entries
- A small "captured on _platform_" line in the entry footer drawn from the `meta.client.platform` field

### Map view

A full-width archive map plots every entry that has coordinates as a milepost glyph dropped on the map. Click any marker to open a popup with the entry's serial, capture time, location, and a brief excerpt of the observation; click "Read entry" in the popup to jump back to the list view with that entry selected. The map view also shows the same detail panel below the map, populated with whatever entry is currently selected. The view meta shows `N of M entries have coordinates` so you can tell at a glance if any entries are missing location data.

Both maps use CartoDB Positron tiles routed through a sepia/desaturation filter to nudge them toward the manila palette of the rest of the instrument. Attribution and controls are restyled to match.

### Loading files

There are three ways to load files into the reader:

1. **Open Folder** uses the File System Access API to read every JSON in a chosen folder at once. Works in Chromium browsers (Chrome, Edge, Brave, Arc).
2. **Open Files** uses the standard file picker. Works everywhere. Select one or many JSON files.
3. **Drag and drop** files or a folder onto the page. Recursive directory drop is supported in most browsers.

The reader loads typography from Google Fonts and the Leaflet map library plus CartoDB tiles from CDN. Entries themselves never leave your machine; there is no upload, no telemetry, no caching beyond the current page session. Clearing the page or hitting the **Clear** button discards the loaded archive without touching any files on disk.

### Per-entry actions

- **View Raw JSON** opens a modal with the full structured payload, useful when you want to verify exactly what was captured
- **Print** uses a stylesheet that hides the sidebar, map controls, and chrome, leaving a clean single-page render of the entry suitable for paper
- **Clear** wipes the loaded archive from memory without touching disk

## Privacy

All data stays on your machine. There is no server. Capture requests go directly from your browser to each data source's public API. API keys live in your browser's localStorage. The instrument carries no analytics, no telemetry, no tracking. Saved files are plain files on your disk.

A modern browser supporting ES2020, Fetch, and CSS Grid. Tested on recent Chrome, Firefox, and Safari. Google Fonts are loaded from CDN; system font fallbacks engage when offline. The page works fully without internet for everything except the actual data fetches.

## Philosophy

Milepost captures a snapshot in time, not a feed. Each entry is a frozen moment with whatever external context was visible from the network at the moment of capture. This frames how data sources are chosen: instead of pursuing stable APIs with version guarantees and rate-limit contracts, the instrument prefers **scraping** in the literal newspaper sense, ripping the page out, taking what is there, accepting that it will not be there forever. Brittleness is acceptable because once an entry is written to disk, the data is preserved. If Wikipedia restructures next year, or Apple deprecates a feed, or Yahoo locks down CORS, today's captured entry still has today's data, frozen on the page. The captures themselves become a record of what was even available to know.

This is why **Wikipedia** is the press wire of the instrument: encyclopedic, daily-updated for popular topics, CORS-friendly via `origin=*`, and structured enough to parse without auth. Wikipedia covers Box Office #1, Billboard Hot 100 #1, Billboard 200 #1, current events, recent deaths, and dozens of other "what is true today" questions. Scrapers prefer Wikipedia first, then specific sources second (Apple Marketing Tools, NOAA, etc.), then nothing if neither responds. The status page tells you which is reachable from your environment.

When a useful source rejects browser-origin requests (Yahoo Finance since Feb 2025, Apple Marketing Tools intermittently, others as conditions change), Milepost routes the request through a **CORS proxy**, a service that fetches the URL server-side and returns it with the right headers. The real-world analog is a clipping service: someone else grabs the newspaper and hands you the cutout. The default proxy is AllOrigins, which is free and unauthenticated; the URL is configurable in Settings, and clearing the field disables proxying. Every fetcher tries direct first; the proxy is only used when direct fails. This means CORS-friendly sources (Wikipedia, NOAA, Open-Meteo, CoinGecko, etc.) never touch the proxy.

**v0.0.24** Predictable PDF filename in the Reader. The Reader's Print action invokes `window.print()`, which presents the browser's print dialog where the user can choose "Save as PDF". Browsers populate the suggested filename from `document.title`, which until now was always "MILEPOST Reader" regardless of which entry was being printed. Result: every saved PDF was named the same, and users had to rename each one manually to keep them organized alongside the corresponding JSON and TXT exports. v0.0.24 swaps the document title to match the TXT/JSON filename pattern (`milepost_YYYYMMDD-HHMM_NNN`) for the duration of the print, then restores the original title via the `afterprint` event so navigation between entries continues to show the proper page name. The PDF saved from print now lines up with the other two file formats: `milepost_20260527-2244_001.pdf` next to `milepost_20260527-2244_001.txt` and `milepost_20260527-2244_001.json`.

**v0.0.23** Persisted milepost serial counter and override. While adding the serial-override feature requested by Mike, an underlying bug surfaced: the serial counter (`STATE.serial`) was never persisted to localStorage. It initialized to 1 on every page load, which meant every browser refresh silently restarted the numbering and any new captures collided with previous numbers from the same browser. The single-session experience was fine (Mike's been capturing in one continuous session for hours), but anyone who closed the tab and came back would find their next capture labeled №001 again. v0.0.23 fixes both at once: the counter now reads from `Settings.get('next_serial', 1)` on init, persists to Settings on each capture, and exposes a "Next Milepost #" input field in the Settings panel for manual override. The override is the use case Mike asked for: if browser data is wiped and you have existing JSON captures up to №247, you set Next to 248 and the next capture picks up there. The keysStatus line in Settings also now shows the current next-serial value (`Next №247`) so you can see at a glance where the counter is without opening Settings. The Export/Import feature from v0.0.22 was extended to include `next_serial` in the JSON, so a settings file round-trips your numbering position along with keys and proxy. Validation: only positive integers accepted; bad input restores the field to current value rather than silently resetting to 1.

**v0.0.22** Settings Export/Import. New Export and Import buttons in the Settings panel let users back up their configuration (API keys, CORS proxy URL, sound preference) to a JSON file and restore it later. Useful for moving config between browsers or devices, recovering from a localStorage wipe (incognito sessions, clearing browser data), or sharing a configuration template (with placeholder keys). The exported file is a small JSON document with `format: "milepost-settings"` and a `version` field for future schema migrations; it carries a `note` field calling out that the file contains API keys and should be treated as sensitive. Import validates the format identifier before writing anything (won't accept arbitrary JSON), accepts partial files (fields missing from the file leave the current setting alone; empty-string fields explicitly clear that setting), and updates both the in-memory Settings store and the visible input fields so the user sees what was loaded. Filenames are timestamped `milepost-settings_YYYY-MM-DDTHH-MM-SS.json`.

**v0.0.21** Bounded probe concurrency to fix the persistent Stream Gauges red. v0.0.20's bbox-matching fix turned out to be a symptom-treatment that didn't address the real problem: the status page was firing all 36 probes in parallel via `Promise.allSettled(SOURCES.map(runOne))`, and the browser's per-origin HTTP/2 connection cap was queueing slow-to-respond upstreams (USGS waterservices, wheretheiss.at, api.wikimedia.org) until they hit per-fetch timeouts. Mike's captures hit USGS as a single request and completed fast, while the probe's USGS request was one of 30+ simultaneous outbound connections and never got bandwidth. v0.0.21 introduces a small concurrency-limited runner that caps simultaneous probes at 6. Total diagnostic time goes from ~5-8 seconds to ~12-18 seconds (roughly 2-3x slower wall clock), but each probe now runs under conditions closer to a real capture: a handful of concurrent requests, not 30. Slow probes that were previously timing out should now complete cleanly; warn-state probes (ISS, NASA APOD) should also resolve faster because they're not contending for connection slots with finance/RSS/Wikipedia fetches that were saturating the browser's connection pool.

**v0.0.20** Stream Gauges probe matched to capture behavior. Mike's v0.0.19 diagnostic showed Stream Gauges failing with "signal is aborted without reason" at 8003ms while his actual captures were getting real USGS data (Wills Creek near Cumberland, 2,470 ft³/s discharge, 5.66 ft gauge height). Tracing the discrepancy: the probe was issuing a substantially larger bbox query than the capture's first attempt (0.5deg half-width vs 0.3deg) and querying only the 00060 discharge parameter instead of the capture's 00060,00065 (discharge AND gauge height). The larger bbox spanned roughly 4x the area, hitting more USGS gauge sites and running into a slower response window. The capture's smaller initial bbox completed fast; the probe's wider one didn't. v0.0.20 matches the probe to the capture's first-attempt query exactly: 0.3deg half-width, 00060,00065 parameter set. Timeout also bumped from 8s to 12s since USGS NWIS is genuinely slow during peak hours and the smaller bbox doesn't help with raw API latency. The probe message also now references "matches capture's first attempt" so future diagnostics make it clear the probe and capture are running the same query.

**v0.0.19** Graceful handling of structurally-unreliable fallback sources, plus a Stream Gauges timeout fix. Mike's v0.0.18 diagnostic was down to three reds: ipapi.co (HTTP 403 from Cloudflare-Worker IP, ipapi.co blocks known cloud-IP ranges as anti-abuse policy), Stream Gauges (USGS waterservices was slow and the probe hit the 30-second HARD_TIMEOUT_MS without a per-fetch timeout to bail earlier), and YouTube Trending (all 6 Piped/Invidious instances unreachable direct AND via the Cloudflare Worker, since the community-instance ecosystem is genuinely unstable and many instances are dead or refusing requests at any given moment). v0.0.19 addresses each: **ipapi.co** now returns __skip on full failure with reasoning that it's only used when browser geo is denied, so when geo is granted (the normal case), this fallback's reachability doesn't affect captures. **Stream Gauges** wraps its fetch in probeFetchWithTimeout(8000) so a slow USGS will fail in 8 seconds instead of holding for 30; if USGS is genuinely down the probe still reports red, but transient slowness doesn't burn the full HARD_TIMEOUT budget. **YouTube Trending** now returns __skip when all phases fail, since the Piped/Invidious mesh is volunteer-run and instances come and go regularly; the capture-side fetcher already omits youtube from the cultural block when no instance responds, so this isn't a regression. The error message also now includes proxy-phase errors (previously only direct errors were displayed). With these changes Mike's diagnostic should reach all-green (modulo whatever transient slowness ISS or USGS exhibit at the moment of probing, which the warn state handles correctly), with skipped probes accurately reporting which fallback paths are inaccessible.

**v0.0.18** location bug discovered and fixed, plus YouTube and ipapi.co routed through proxy. **The location bug**: Mike noticed the Reader's stats banner showing 0 locations even with many captured entries. Investigation revealed that the reverse-geocoding endpoint Milepost was calling, `https://geocoding-api.open-meteo.com/v1/reverse`, **doesn't exist**. Open-Meteo's geocoding API is forward-only at `/v1/search`; the reverse path silently 404'd, the try-catch swallowed the failure, and every capture stored `location.place: null`. The Reader correctly counted only entries with non-null place names, finding none. Confirmed via web search and Open-Meteo's own GitHub discussions where the team explicitly recommends Nominatim (OpenStreetMap) for reverse geocoding. v0.0.18 switches to Nominatim's `/reverse` endpoint with `zoom=10` for city-level granularity, parses the `address` block for the best available locality name (city, town, village, hamlet, municipality, suburb in order of preference), and falls back to Nominatim's `display_name` if those are all missing. New captures now store proper place names. **Reader-side improvement**: for entries already captured before v0.0.18 (which all have place: null), the Reader's location counter now falls back to bucketing by rounded lat/lon coordinates. Existing entries still count as distinct locations in the banner. **YouTube via proxy**: with Mike's Cloudflare Worker now live, the YouTube fetcher can take advantage of it for instances unreachable from his network. v0.0.18 adopts a two-phase strategy: Phase 1 tries direct on each of 9 instances with a 3-second timeout (fast path when any direct works); Phase 2, triggered only if all direct attempts fail, routes the first two instances through the configured proxy via fetchScrape. The status probe mirrors this with a trimmed 6-instance list to stay under the 30-second HARD_TIMEOUT. **ipapi.co via proxy**: same pattern; the IP geolocation fallback now goes through fetchScrape (capture side) and probeFetch (status side) so it can ride the configured proxy when direct is blocked. **corsproxy.io fallback probe**: now returns __skip instead of throwing when corsproxy.io is unreachable. This proxy is the secondary in the dual-proxy chain and is only ever called when the primary fails; when users have a working primary (especially a personal Cloudflare Worker), corsproxy.io's reachability doesn't actually affect captures. The probe still tests it and reports the reason if it fails, but as a skipped probe rather than a failure, so it doesn't count against the all-green target.

**v0.0.17** Cloudflare Worker proxy and accurate transport probes. The v0.0.16 diagnostic exports kept showing AllOrigins as the proxy under test, even after deploying a personal Cloudflare Worker, because the "CORS proxy (AllOrigins)" probe in `milepost-status.html` was hardcoded to test AllOrigins specifically, regardless of what was actually configured in Settings. The transport row of the diagnostic was lying about the transport. **v0.0.17 fixes this** by replacing the single hardcoded probe with two dynamic probes: **"CORS proxy (primary, configured)"** reads `milepost_cors_proxy` from localStorage and probes whatever's set there (default still AllOrigins for first-run users; whatever Worker URL you've configured otherwise); **"CORS proxy (fallback, corsproxy.io)"** probes the hardcoded secondary that `fetchScrape` falls to when primary fails. Both probes ask the proxy to fetch a small known-good Wikipedia summary endpoint and verify pass-through by checking the response's `title` field. Result messages now include the actual hostname under test, e.g. `milepost-proxy.YOUR-NAME.workers.dev reachable, pass-through returned "HTTP"` so the diagnostic export tells the truth about which proxy is doing the work. Also shipped alongside: `cloudflare-worker-proxy.js`, a 50-line self-contained Worker script that deploys in under a minute to Cloudflare's free tier and gives users their own CORS proxy with no third-party blocklist. The README's new "Self-hosting a CORS proxy" section walks through the seven-step deployment. With your own Worker in place, the persistent holdouts (Yahoo, Stooq, FRED, Frankfurter, Apple feeds, arXiv) should all come through because your Worker has no upstream-host policy to fight.

**v0.0.16** focus slider fix and YouTube instance walk reshaping. Two issues surfaced from Mike's deployed-and-tested v0.0.15. **The focus slider bug**: Mike reported that the Focus value from capture wasn't appearing in the reader. The Mood/Energy/Focus sliders use a `dataset.set='1'` flag to distinguish "user actually interacted" from "left at default", and that flag was only being set on the `input` event. The `input` event fires when the slider value *changes*. If the user clicks on the slider but doesn't drag it (because the value they want is already 5, the midpoint, or because they tapped without moving), no input event fires and capture records `null` for that slider. Reader's renderer then correctly hides the row because `val == null` returns empty. In practice this hit Focus more than Mood/Energy probably because Mike's typical focus value is around the midpoint, where small adjustments don't change the integer step. **The fix** adds a `pointerdown` listener alongside the existing `input` listener: any first touch on the slider marks it set, even without value change. Keyboard users still trigger the input event on arrow keys so they're unaffected. **YouTube instance walk**: Mike's diagnostic showed the YouTube probe timing out at 30000ms with all 9 instances exhausted, because each instance was running through the full `probeFetch` chain (direct → AllOrigins → corsproxy.io) at 25 seconds each, meaning only 1-2 instances got attempted within the 30s HARD_TIMEOUT cap. Piped and Invidious instances are CORS-friendly in theory, so when one is reachable, direct works fast. The fix tightens the per-instance budget to 3 seconds of direct-only attempts, allowing the entire 9-instance list to be walked in ~27 seconds. If none work direct, we no longer fall through to the proxy chain (it was thrashing AllOrigins serializing 18 sequential proxy requests per capture for diminishing returns). Both the main capture fetcher and the status probe get the same shortened budget. **About the corsproxy.io 403s**: Mike's diagnostic also surfaced that corsproxy.io is returning HTTP 403 for Yahoo, Stooq, Frankfurter, Apple, and arXiv. This isn't a bug we can fix in code: corsproxy.io maintains a blocklist of "API-like" upstream hosts to prevent their service being used as a free pass-through to commercial APIs, and our financial-data and chart-feed hosts fall on that list. AllOrigins still works for those (when it's not under load); FRED came through in 181ms via AllOrigins on this run. The dual-proxy fallback from v0.0.15 means we get the union of what each proxy permits, but not 100% coverage; for the holdouts the answer is either tolerating intermittent gaps or self-hosting a CORS proxy via Cloudflare Workers (free tier, ~50 lines of code, no third-party dependency at all). Documenting that path in a future README section is on the roadmap.

**v0.0.15** dual-proxy fallback and per-fetch timeouts. Mike's second diagnostic export from v0.0.14 surfaced a new failure mode: all 7 proxy-bound probes timed out at exactly 12000ms (the status page's HARD_TIMEOUT_MS cap), and the AllOrigins ping itself came back in 1207ms instead of the previous 24ms. AllOrigins, the lone CORS proxy v0.0.10 introduced, was degraded that afternoon; every fetch routed through it was hanging well past the 12-second budget. With only one proxy in the chain, a single bad day at AllOrigins meant the entire Markets / Cultural / Curio block of CORS-blocked sources went dark in real captures, not just on the status page. The architectural fix is twofold. **A hardcoded secondary proxy** `corsproxy.io` ships alongside the user's configured primary (default still AllOrigins). When the primary stalls or returns non-OK, `fetchScrape` automatically falls through to corsproxy.io before giving up. The two services use different infrastructure and rarely degrade simultaneously. The user's primary stays user-configurable; the fallback is internal so the chain is reliable even when someone clears the proxy field or sets a custom URL. **Per-fetch AbortController timeouts** prevent any single proxy hang from pinning the whole pipeline. Direct fetches get 5 seconds (CORS-friendly sources land here fast; CORS-blocked ones fail-fast as before); each proxy gets 10 seconds. Total worst-case for any `fetchScrape` call is now 25 seconds (5 direct + 10 primary + 10 fallback), bounded instead of unbounded. The status page's `HARD_TIMEOUT_MS` is bumped from 12 to 30 seconds to accommodate this with headroom, and `probeFetch` is refactored identically so the status page mirrors the capture transport exactly. The path-reporting in probe results is upgraded from `(via direct)` / `(via proxy)` to `(via direct)` / `(via proxy:api.allorigins.win)` / `(via proxy:corsproxy.io)` so the diagnostic export shows which proxy actually delivered the data. Now: if AllOrigins is fast, results route through it. If AllOrigins is slow, results route through corsproxy.io after 10 seconds. If both proxies are down, the error message names both. The capture instrument inherits all of this since it uses the same `fetchScrape` helper for everything CORS-fragile.

**v0.0.12** DJIA close from FRED, replacing the misleading milestone. v0.0.11 added a Wikipedia-scraped "DJIA Milestone Close" which captured the most recent round-number threshold the index had crossed (e.g., "first close above 50,000 on Feb 6, 2026"). That was a category mistake for a journaling tool: a Milepost entry should record the world's state at the moment of the entry, not the most recent point at which the index hit a milestone (which can lag by weeks or months). v0.0.12 swaps that fetcher for `getDjiaCurrent()`, which pulls the actual daily close from **FRED** (Federal Reserve Economic Data, run by the St. Louis Fed). FRED publishes the official DJIA close daily after market close (~7pm CST), sourced directly from S&P Dow Jones Indices, and exposes it as a public CSV download at `fred.stlouisfed.org/graph/fredgraph.csv?id=DJIA` with no API key required. The fetcher requests the last 30 days (~600 bytes of CSV), walks from the end of the file, skips FRED's "." placeholder for missing values (weekends and holidays), and returns the most recent valid close with its date. Stored at `data.markets.djiaClose` with `closeValue`, `closeDate` (human-formatted: "May 26, 2026"), `closeDateISO` (machine-readable: "2026-05-26"), and `source: 'FRED'`. Routed through `fetchScrape` so it goes direct first and falls back to the CORS proxy if needed; FRED has CORS enabled on most endpoints so direct usually works. Text export shows "DJIA Close" at the top of MARKETS with the value, date, and source line. Reader shows it as the first row with the date as the qualifier. Status page swapped the Wikipedia milestone probe for a FRED probe that actually fetches the CSV and reports back the most recent close and date so users can see exactly what's coming back. This is now the *authoritative* DJIA data point in a capture: when Yahoo and Stooq fail (which happens in many environments), FRED still gives you the real close. When Yahoo works, both values appear side by side; FRED is the daily close, Yahoo is the intraday or last trade.

**v0.0.10** universal scraping via CORS proxy. The instrument's fragile sources (Yahoo Finance, Apple Marketing Tools, Stooq, Frankfurter) have been quietly failing in many environments because browsers refuse cross-origin requests to hosts that don't set `Access-Control-Allow-Origin: *`. With this in place, all the fetchers that have been silently failing should now succeed (or fail with a clear diagnostic indicating both direct and proxy paths were exhausted), and the instrument is durable against future CORS-policy tightening at other sources without requiring a code change for each.

**v0.0.8.1** bug fixes for movie parser and stock-market fetcher. Two long-standing issues surfaced after watching real captures. **The Wikipedia movie parser was pulling truncated and incorrect data**, displaying entries like "Top Movie / Star Trek / $75" instead of the actual #1 film. The cause was twofold: the film-name extraction grabbed the LAST italic-wrapped wiki-link in the entire article, which sometimes matched a film reference inside a "Notes" column ("broke Star Trek's record (...)") rather than the real Film cell; and the gross regex `\$([0-9](?:[0-9,]*[0-9])?)` did not include decimals or require comma groups, so a notes mention like "$75.4 million" was captured as just "$75". Fix: replaced the parser with a row-based walk that splits the wikitext on `|-` row delimiters, then scans each row's cells in order to find the FILM cell (first italic link) followed by the GROSS cell (first dollar amount with comma groups). The gross regex `\$(\d{1,3}(?:,\d{3})+)(?!\d|\.\d)` now requires at least one comma group, which filters out all the "$X million" / "$X.X million" shorthand references that appear in notes. A loose-scan fallback remains for the case where the article structure ever changes. **The Markets section was empty because Yahoo Finance disabled CORS for browser-origin requests as part of their February 2025 redesign**, which silently broke all stock, treasury, and commodity fetchers. The fix adds Stooq as a fallback: `getStockIndex(symbol)` now tries Yahoo first (richer schema with true previous close) and on any failure falls back to Stooq's free CSV endpoint at `stooq.com/q/l/`, which is CORS-enabled. Yahoo's symbols map to Stooq's lowercase indices (`^GSPC` → `^spx`, `^DJI` → `^dji`, `^IXIC` → `^ndq`, `^VIX` → `^vix`, `^TNX` → `^tnx`, etc.) and dot-f futures notation (`GC=F` → `gc.f`, `CL=F` → `cl.f`, `SI=F` → `si.f`). Stooq's lite endpoint gives OHLCV for the current trading day but no separate previous close, so the day's change is computed as (close - open), which is the intraday change rather than vs-previous-close, but still gives the directional signal. Both sources tag their data with a `source: "yahoo" | "stooq"` field on the saved snapshot so you can tell which one was used. Diagnostic console messages (`[Milepost] markets/yahoo` and `markets/stooq`) make failures visible. The self-status page gained a separate Stooq probe row alongside the Yahoo one so you can see which sources are reachable from your environment.
